Netherlands Legal Term

Koopakte

Dutch: Koopakte (Purchase Agreement)

The binding written purchase agreement in Dutch property law, signed between buyer and seller before the notary deed. Once signed, the buyer has a 3-day statutory cooling-off period.

TL;DRThe binding written purchase agreement in Dutch property law, signed between buyer and seller before the notary deed. Once signed, the buyer has a 3-day statutory cooling-off period.

The koopakte (purchase agreement) is the preliminary binding contract in a Dutch residential property transaction, signed after an offer is verbally accepted but before the final notarial deed (leveringsakte). It sets out the agreed purchase price, the transfer date, all conditions (ontbindende voorwaarden), and any inventory items included in the sale. Both parties are legally bound from the moment of signing.

Dutch law grants residential buyers a statutory three-day cooling-off period (bedenktijd) after signing the koopakte. During these three days — which must include at least two working days — the buyer may withdraw unconditionally without penalty and recover any deposit paid. This right applies only to private buyers, not to investors purchasing as a company.

The most critical clauses in the koopakte are the ontbindende voorwaarden (resolutive conditions). The most common are: financieringsvoorbehoud (subject to mortgage approval, typically within four to six weeks), bouwkundige keuring (subject to satisfactory structural survey), and Nationale Hypotheek Garantie (NHG) eligibility. If these conditions cannot be met, the buyer may withdraw without penalty.

After the koopakte is signed, both parties must appear before a notaris (notary) — typically within four to eight weeks — for the overdracht (transfer). The notaris verifies title, registers the transfer in the Kadaster (land registry), and ensures all mortgage deeds are signed simultaneously. The notaris is neutral; buyers should separately engage a makelaar (estate agent) or jurist to review the koopakte before signing.

For expats, one practical note: the koopakte is always in Dutch. You are entitled to request a translation or bring an interpreter, but the Dutch text is legally authoritative. Do not rely on verbal explanations of what the document says — have a bilingual professional review it before signing.

⚠ Common MistakeSigning the koopakte without a financieringsvoorbehoud clause if you need a mortgage. Without this clause, failing to secure financing does not release you from the contract — you lose your 10% deposit and may face further damages claims.
💡 Expert TipUse the three-day cooling-off period actively: commission a bouwkundige keuring (structural survey) immediately after signing the koopakte. Most surveyors can turn around a report within 48 hours. If major defects are found, you can withdraw within the cooling-off window at no cost.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens if the seller pulls out after signing the koopakte?

The buyer is entitled to specific performance (nakoming) — a Dutch court can force the sale to proceed — or damages equivalent to at least 10% of the purchase price. Sellers who withdraw face significant financial exposure.

Can I negotiate the ontbindende voorwaarden after signing?

No — the conditions are fixed at signing. This is why it is critical to include all necessary conditions (mortgage, survey, permit checks) before you sign, not afterwards.

Is the koopakte registered anywhere officially?

The koopakte itself is not registered publicly. Only the final leveringsakte (transfer deed) is registered in the Kadaster. However, the koopakte is often noted in the Kadaster as a koop-aanmerkingstekening to protect the buyer against the seller disposing of the property to a third party.
