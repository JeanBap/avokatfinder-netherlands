Overdrachtsbelasting

Overdrachtsbelasting is the Netherlands' property transfer tax — 2% for owner-occupied homes, 10.4% for all other property types including buy-to-let and commercial.

What Is Overdrachtsbelasting?

Overdrachtsbelasting (transfer tax) is the Dutch tax levied when ownership of immovable property changes hands. It is calculated on the purchase price (or market value if higher) and is payable by the buyer. The rate has been reformed significantly since 2021 and now applies three different rates depending on the buyer's situation and property use.

The Three-Rate System

Since 1 January 2021, the Netherlands applies: 0% for qualifying first-time buyers aged 18–34 purchasing a property up to a value ceiling (€510,000 in 2024) as their primary residence — a full exemption; 2% for buyers purchasing a property as their primary and permanent residence (regardless of age, if not using the 0% exemption); and 10.4% for all other acquisitions — investment properties, second homes, commercial real estate, and property acquired by legal entities.

First-Time Buyer Exemption Details

The 0% rate for first-time buyers requires: the buyer is between 18 and 34 years old; they have not previously used this exemption; the property price does not exceed the ceiling (adjusted annually by the government); and the buyer will use the property as their primary residence. The exemption is per person — a couple where only one qualifies may split the transfer tax (0% on one half, 2% on the other). Both must sign a notarial statement confirming eligibility.

Buy-to-Let Impact

The 10.4% rate on investment property purchases (effective January 2023, up from 8%) significantly increased the cost of buy-to-let investment in the Netherlands. On a €350,000 rental property, the transfer tax alone is €36,400 — materially affecting investment yields and effectively deterring small investors. This was a deliberate government policy to reduce competition for housing between investors and owner-occupiers.

Calculation and Payment

The notary calculates overdrachtsbelasting based on the deed value and collects it from the buyer on the day of transfer. It is remitted to the Belastingdienst (Tax Authority). The notary also handles the required declarations (for the 0% or 2% rate) — both buyer and notary sign these, as the notary is legally responsible for applying the correct rate.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Applying the 2% rate to a property you intend to rent out immediately. Investment property is 10.4% — misclassification is treated as tax fraud by the Belastingdienst.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: If buying with a partner where only one qualifies for the 0% first-buyer rate, structure the ownership split to maximise the exemption — a 50/50 split means 0% on one half and 2% on the other, saving up to €1,275 on a €510,000 property.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the price ceiling for the 0% rate?

It is adjusted annually — verify the current ceiling with your notary as it changes each January.

Does the 10.4% apply to parking spaces?

Yes — standalone parking purchases are treated as property and attract 10.4% if not part of a primary residence purchase.

Can a company use the 2% rate?

No — legal entities always pay 10.4% regardless of intended use.
