Netherlands Legal Term

Hypotheekakte

Dutch: Hypotheekakte (Mortgage Deed)

The notarised Dutch mortgage deed that creates a registered lien (hypotheek) over a property in favour of the lender. Signed simultaneously with the leveringsakte (transfer deed) at the notaris.

TL;DRThe notarised Dutch mortgage deed that creates a registered lien (hypotheek) over a property in favour of the lender. Signed simultaneously with the leveringsakte (transfer deed) at the notaris.

The hypotheekakte is the formal notarised document that creates a hypotheek (mortgage charge) over Dutch property as security for a loan. It must be signed before a notaris and registered in the Kadaster to be legally effective. Without Kadaster registration, the charge does not exist as a property right — it is merely a personal contractual obligation, far weaker as security.

In a typical Dutch property purchase with mortgage financing, the buyer signs two notarial deeds on the same day: the leveringsakte (transfer deed, which transfers ownership) and the hypotheekakte (mortgage deed, which charges the newly acquired property to the lender). The notaris disburses the mortgage funds simultaneously with the transfer, ensuring the lender's security is in place from the first moment of the buyer's ownership.

The hypotheekakte specifies the maximum secured amount (hypotheeksommedrag) — typically 110–120% of the actual loan amount to cover potential interest arrears and enforcement costs. This "overcollateralisation" means the Kadaster shows a higher charge than the actual loan balance. If you later try to borrow more against the same property, lenders will see the Kadaster encumbrance and factor it into their lending decision.

Dutch mortgage law provides borrowers with strong protections against aggressive enforcement. A lender cannot simply sell a property on default — they must obtain a court order (executoriale titel) unless the hypotheekakte contains a specific clause granting parate executie (immediate enforcement right without a court order). Most Dutch mortgage deeds include this clause, so read it carefully.

When a Dutch mortgage is fully repaid, the hypotheek does not automatically disappear from the Kadaster. The lender must execute a notarised doorhaling (cancellation deed) to remove it. This typically costs €200–500 in notaris fees and is the lender's responsibility to initiate — though in practice, borrowers often need to chase their bank. A Kadaster encumbrance from a repaid mortgage can complicate future sales or refinancing.

⚠ Common MistakeForgetting to request a doorhaling (cancellation) after fully repaying a Dutch mortgage. An old hypotheek remaining on the Kadaster from a previous loan can block refinancing or complicate a future sale — and the bank is not always proactive about removing it.
💡 Expert TipAsk your mortgage lender for a written confirmation of the exact hypotheeksommedrag (maximum secured amount) before signing. If you plan to make overpayments or have a repayment vehicle mortgage, understanding the Kadaster registration amount helps you plan future borrowing capacity against the same property.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I have two mortgages (two hypotheekaktes) on the same Dutch property?

Yes — multiple hypotheken can be registered on the same property, ranked by priority (eerste hypotheek, tweede hypotheek). In enforcement, proceeds are distributed to lenders in order of Kadaster registration date. Second-charge lenders charge higher rates to reflect their subordinate position.

What is NHG and does it affect the hypotheekakte?

NHG (Nationale Hypotheek Garantie) is a government-backed mortgage guarantee for loans below a set threshold (€435,000 in 2024). If the lender has NHG backing, this is noted in the hypotheekakte and affects enforcement procedures. NHG mortgages offer lower interest rates in exchange for borrower obligations including insurance requirements.

If I buy out my co-owner from a jointly owned Dutch property, does the hypotheekakte change?

The transfer of co-ownership requires a new leveringsakte. If the mortgage was in both names, the lender must agree to release the departing co-owner from liability — this may require refinancing. The notaris handles the simultaneous ownership transfer and mortgage restructuring in a single appointment.
