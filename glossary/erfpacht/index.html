Netherlands Legal Term

Erfpacht

Dutch: Erfpacht (Ground Lease)

A Dutch ground lease arrangement where the landowner (often a municipality) retains ownership of the land while the building owner pays annual ground rent (canon). Common in Amsterdam, Utrecht, and The Hague.

TL;DRA Dutch ground lease arrangement where the landowner (often a municipality) retains ownership of the land while the building owner pays annual ground rent (canon). Common in Amsterdam, Utrecht, and The Hague.

Erfpacht (ground lease) is a property right under Dutch law where one party (the erfpachter) has the right to use and enjoy a plot of land owned by another party (the bloot eigenaar, typically a municipality or private landowner) in exchange for annual ground rent (canon). The erfpachter owns the buildings on the land but not the land itself. This arrangement is extremely common in Amsterdam, where a significant proportion of properties — including many highly desirable canal houses — sit on municipal ground lease land.

The key financial risk in erfpacht lies in canon revision clauses. Most ground leases specify that the canon will be revised at fixed intervals — commonly every 50 or 75 years, but sometimes every 10 years. Revisions are based on the then-current land value, which in Amsterdam and other major cities has increased dramatically. Buyers who purchase an erfpacht property without fully understanding the revision schedule and methodology can face canon increases of several thousand euros per year at the next revision date.

Amsterdam's municipality controversially switched from perpetual ground leases (eeuwigdurende erfpacht, with canons fixed at purchase) to continuous ground leases (voortdurende erfpacht, with periodic revisions) for new plots from 2017. Owners of older properties with perpetual ground leases have the option to switch to the new system — a decision that requires careful financial modelling, as the switch can lock in a canon level before a market-value revision would otherwise apply.

When purchasing an erfpacht property, always obtain a copy of the erfpachtakte (ground lease deed) from the notaris. Key terms to examine: the canon amount and next revision date, the revision methodology (fixed index, land value, or negotiated), the duration of the lease, the conditions for renewal or termination, and any building restrictions imposed by the landowner.

Mortgage lenders in the Netherlands are generally willing to finance erfpacht properties, but they may impose conditions: the ground lease must have a remaining term of at least 30 years beyond the mortgage term, and some lenders require the lease to have NHG (national mortgage guarantee) approval. A small number of lenders decline erfpacht properties entirely, reducing your financing options.

⚠ Common MistakeNot checking the next canon revision date before buying an erfpacht property. A property with a revision due in two years carries entirely different financial risk from one revised 40 years ago with 30 years remaining. The revision could multiply your annual ground rent — dramatically affecting affordability.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I buy out the erfpacht and own the land outright?

In some cases yes — depending on the landowner's policy. Amsterdam municipality has historically offered buy-out options at specified rates, though these have become more expensive as land values rose. Private landowners may or may not be willing to sell. A buy-out is called afkoop van de canon and is handled through the notaris.

Does erfpacht affect the property's resale value?

Yes — properties on ground lease typically sell at a discount to equivalent freehold properties, reflecting the ongoing canon obligation and revision uncertainty. The discount varies widely by lease terms and location. Properties with imminent revision dates or unfavourable terms can be significantly discounted.

Is erfpacht unique to the Netherlands?

No — similar ground lease systems exist in other countries (Erbpacht in Germany, leasehold in the UK). However, the Dutch erfpacht system, particularly Amsterdam's municipal ground lease, is one of the most extensive in Europe in terms of the proportion of urban properties affected.
