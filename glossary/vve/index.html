Netherlands Legal Term

VvE (Vereniging van Eigenaren)

Dutch: Vereniging van Eigenaren (Owners' Association)

The mandatory owners' association in Dutch apartment buildings that manages common areas, maintains a reserve fund, and enforces building rules. Every apartment owner is automatically a member.

TL;DRThe mandatory owners' association in Dutch apartment buildings that manages common areas, maintains a reserve fund, and enforces building rules. Every apartment owner is automatically a member.

A Vereniging van Eigenaren (VvE — Owners' Association) is the mandatory legal entity in any Dutch building divided into apartment rights (appartementsrechten). Membership is automatic upon purchasing any apartment — you cannot opt out. The VvE owns the common areas collectively, maintains the building, and administers shared costs through a monthly service charge (servicekosten or VvE-bijdrage).

Dutch law requires every active VvE to maintain a minimum reserve fund (reservefonds) sufficient to cover major maintenance costs. Since 2018, the minimum legal requirement is 0.5% of the building's rebuild value per year (or a maintenance plan-based alternative). A healthy reserve fund indicates good management; a zero or minimal fund means future special levies (extra bijdragen) for major works will fall entirely on current owners at the time.

Before purchasing an apartment in a VvE building, buyers should request: the last three years of VvE meeting minutes (notulen), the current annual budget, the reserve fund balance, the multi-year maintenance plan (MJOP), and confirmation that all current owners are up to date with their service charges. These documents reveal the true financial health of the building — far more informative than the apartment's condition alone.

A VvE that is "slapend" (dormant) — existing on paper but not actively meeting, collecting contributions, or maintaining a reserve — is a serious red flag. Dutch mortgage lenders increasingly refuse to finance apartments in slapende VvE buildings, and owners can face forced activation with potentially large catch-up contributions for deferred maintenance.

Voting at VvE meetings is weighted by the breukdeel (fraction) of each owner's share — similar to millesimi in French copropriétés. Major decisions (renovation, changing the VvE rules, significant expenditure) require qualified majorities. As a foreign owner who cannot attend Dutch-language meetings, granting a proxy to a trusted Dutch-speaking owner or property manager is a practical necessity.

⚠ Common MistakeNot asking for the reservefonds balance before making an offer. A Dutch apartment with a €0 reserve fund in a building that needs a new roof, lift, or facade repairs means you will face a large one-off special levy shortly after purchase — costs that should be reflected in a reduced offer price.
💡 Expert TipRequest the Meerjarenonderhoudsplan (MJOP — multi-year maintenance plan) as well as the reserve fund statement. The MJOP projects all anticipated major works for the next 10–30 years and whether the current contributions are sufficient to fund them. Any gap between projected costs and reserves is your future liability.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a breukdeel and how does it affect my costs?

The breukdeel is your fractional share of the building's common areas — similar to shares in a company. It determines your vote weight and your proportion of shared costs. A larger apartment on an upper floor typically has a higher breukdeel and therefore pays more in monthly contributions and special levies.

Can the VvE prevent me from renting out my apartment?

The VvE's splitsingsreglement (division rules) may contain restrictions on subletting, particularly for short-term platforms like Airbnb. Some Amsterdam buildings have voted blanket prohibitions on tourist rentals. Check the reglement carefully before purchasing for rental purposes.

What happens if the VvE decides on major works I cannot afford?

VvE decisions are binding on all members once a sufficient majority votes in favour. You are obliged to pay your share of the levy. If you cannot pay, the VvE can pursue enforcement action — ultimately including a forced sale of your apartment. Always model worst-case special levies before purchasing in a building with deferred maintenance.
