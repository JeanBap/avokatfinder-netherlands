Netherlands Legal Term

Kadaster

Dutch: Kadaster (Land Registry)

The Dutch national land registry and mapping agency that records ownership of all real property, mortgages, and encumbrances. A Kadaster extract (uittreksel) is essential due diligence for any Dutch property purchase.

TL;DRThe Dutch national land registry and mapping agency that records ownership of all real property, mortgages, and encumbrances. A Kadaster extract (uittreksel) is essential due diligence for any Dutch property purchase.

The Kadaster is the Netherlands' national organisation responsible for maintaining the public register of all real estate ownership, mortgages (hypotheken), rights of way, ground leases (erfpacht), and other encumbrances. Every plot of land and building in the Netherlands has a unique Kadaster registration number, and all transfers of ownership and new mortgages must be registered here to be legally effective against third parties.

A Kadaster uittreksel (extract) is the official document showing who owns a property, what mortgages are registered against it, and what other encumbrances apply. This document is publicly available for a small fee (around €3–10 online via kadaster.nl) and is essential due diligence before purchasing any Dutch property. Your notaris will obtain this automatically, but buyers can and should check it independently.

The Kadaster also maintains the Basisregistratie Adressen en Gebouwen (BAG) — a national database of all buildings and addresses in the Netherlands, recording construction year, permitted use, and floor area. Cross-referencing the BAG entry for a property against what the seller represents is valuable due diligence, particularly for properties that have been converted or extended.

For properties on erfpacht (ground lease) land — common in Amsterdam, Utrecht, and other cities — the Kadaster will show the erfpacht right rather than full ownership. Ground lease conditions, including annual canon (ground rent) and revision dates, are not visible in the Kadaster extract itself but are in the underlying erfpacht deed, which your notaris can retrieve. Ground lease properties require significantly more due diligence than freehold.

After the leveringsakte (transfer deed) is signed before the notaris, the notaris files it with the Kadaster within a few hours via an electronic connection. The new ownership is registered almost immediately. This speed of registration is one of the strengths of the Dutch system — protecting buyers from any late-arising claims against the seller.

⚠ Common MistakeNot checking the Kadaster for registered encumbrances beyond the mortgage. Older Dutch properties may have erfdienstbaarheden (easements) — rights of way for neighbours, cable/pipe routes, or building restrictions — that do not appear in estate agent listings but are visible in the Kadaster and bind all future owners.
💡 Expert TipOrder your own Kadaster uittreksel (€3 online) for any property you are seriously considering, before making a formal offer. It tells you exactly who owns the property, what mortgages are outstanding, and what encumbrances apply — information that affects both your offer price and your financing plans.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the Kadaster the same as the GBA (population register)?

No — the Kadaster records property ownership; the GBA (now BRP) records people's addresses. They are separate systems. Registering your address in the BRP does not update the Kadaster; only a notarial deed does.

Can I search the Kadaster for who owns a specific property?

Yes — the Kadaster is a public register. You can search by address or Kadaster number at kadaster.nl and pay a small fee to see the current owner, registered mortgages, and encumbrances. There is no privacy exemption; property ownership is public information in the Netherlands.

What if the Kadaster shows a different owner than the seller?

This is a serious red flag. Stop the transaction immediately and consult a lawyer. Either the Kadaster is not yet updated (possible if a recent transfer is pending registration), or someone other than the listed owner is attempting to sell the property — a potential fraud situation.
